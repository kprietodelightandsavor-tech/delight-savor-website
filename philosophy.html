The Foundation

Charlotte Mason's
Science of Relations

Charlotte Mason called it the "science of relations" — the idea that education is fundamentally about connecting a student to the world of ideas, to people, to history, to beauty, to the great questions of being human. Not filling them with facts. Not training them for tests. Connecting them.

For Mason, the mind feeds on ideas the way the body feeds on food. A dry summary, a watered-down textbook, a packaged lesson — these are the nutritional equivalent of processed food. They keep you going, but they don't nourish.

A living book is different. It's written by someone who actually cared about their subject — someone with something urgent to say. That urgency is what the reader catches. That's what lingers.

"Education is an atmosphere, a discipline, a life."

— Charlotte Mason

What This Looks Like

In a Delight & Savor class — co-op room or kitchen table — this philosophy shapes everything:

  • We read whole, unabridged texts — Macbeth, Wuthering Heights, the actual Canterbury Tales — not summaries or excerpts.
  • Students narrate before they analyze — they say what they see before anyone tells them what to think.
  • We bring ideas into conversation with ideas: a novel alongside a philosopher, a poem alongside a question.
  • I trust students to do the work of forming their own understanding. I don't pre-digest it for them — I build pathways in.

Living Books

Why the Book Itself Matters

A living book is written by someone who loved their subject. A textbook is written by committee. The difference is not just aesthetic — it is formative.

A Typical Textbook Approach

  • Pre-digests ideas so students receive conclusions, not the thinking that led there
  • Summarizes plot and theme rather than letting students encounter the text directly
  • Measures retention through multiple-choice and fill-in-the-blank
  • Treats literature as a vehicle for vocabulary drills and comprehension checks
  • Keeps students at a safe, managed distance from the book

The Living Books Approach

  • Puts students face-to-face with an author's actual thinking and conviction
  • Requires students to form their own understanding through narration and discussion
  • Measures depth through oral narration, written response, and real project work
  • Treats literature as a conversation worth entering — not content to be processed
  • Trusts students to rise to the challenge of a real, demanding book

Narration

The Oldest Assessment Tool
Is Also the Best One

Before written exams, before Scantron sheets — there was narration. A student who has truly understood something can tell it back. Not recite it. Tell it — in their own words, with their own emphasis, with the details that struck them.

Charlotte Mason used narration as her primary form of assessment because she understood something modern education has largely forgotten: the act of telling is itself an act of learning. When a student narrates, they're not just reporting what they read. They're organizing it, selecting what matters, forming it into language — and in doing so, making it their own.

In my curriculum, narration is the backbone. Every week, students narrate orally and in writing. They learn to say what they notice before they're asked what it means. They practice the discipline of attending carefully — to a sentence, a scene, an argument — before they practice the craft of responding to it.

And here's what I've seen in real classrooms: students who struggle with traditional essays often flourish when they're given permission to just tell what happened — what they noticed, what surprised them, what stayed with them. That's not a lower bar. That's a better door in.

"Narration is not a simple exercise in memory. It is an act of mind — a synthesis that requires the student to have actually met the text."

— Kim, Delight & Savor

Writing Philosophy

Ideas Before Craft

Good writing instruction teaches the rules of craft. Great writing instruction asks: what are you trying to say, and why does it matter? Students must have something worth saying before we hand them the tools to say it beautifully.

I don't begin with the five-paragraph essay. I begin with: what did you notice? What surprised you? What question does this raise? When students have something real to say, the architecture of argument becomes meaningful rather than arbitrary.

This is especially important for students who've been told they're "not writers." Often what they're missing isn't skill — it's permission to have an opinion, and a structure that helps them form one. That's what narration builds. That's what the Great Conversation opens up.

The goal isn't a well-structured paragraph about nothing. It's a real thought, shaped with care.

— Kim, Delight & Savor

Narrate first. Respond second. Argue third.

Students move through a progression — from retelling what they saw, to forming a response, to building a full argument — so that each stage of writing is grounded in real engagement with the text.

Scaffolded for every student.

I build every unit so that a student who has always struggled with writing — or who's never thought of themselves as a reader — has a real pathway in. Not easier content. Better structure. More support at the entry points, more freedom as confidence builds.

The Great Conversation

Literature in Conversation
with the Great Thinkers

I don't just teach novels — I set them in conversation with the thinkers who help us understand them. These connections grow and deepen each year. What's here is just a glimpse of an ongoing conversation.

Augustine & Ordered Love

Augustine's ordo amoris — rightly ordered love — gives students a lens for every text. When Macbeth murders his king, he has disordered his loves. When Heathcliff destroys everything around him, he is loving wrongly. Students do philosophy through literature — which is exactly what literature is for.

Aristotle & Virtue

Aristotle's insight that character is formed through habituated action — not just intellectual assent — gives students a second lens: what is this character becoming? We're not moralizing. We're doing serious literary thinking, grounded in one of the oldest frameworks for understanding human nature.

Rousseau & Nature

Rousseau's belief in the natural goodness of humanity — and the corruption wrought by civilization — runs through Gothic literature. Reading Wuthering Heights alongside Rousseau, students suddenly see Heathcliff differently. These aren't just themes. They're living arguments, and students learn to take a position on them.

In coming years, we'll be adding Charlotte Mason herself, Simone Weil, and others to this conversation. The canon I'm building with students isn't fixed — it grows alongside the curriculum.

Why I Built This

Nine Years in a Classroom.
Then I Came Home.

I spent nine years teaching English in public schools — AP Language & Composition, AP British Literature, honors courses. I loved it. I was good at it. And I knew, the whole time, that something was missing.

The curriculum we were given was efficient. Measurable. It covered the standards. But it was not, in Mason's sense, alive. We were processing literature rather than living inside it. Students learned to produce the right kind of paragraph. They did not learn to linger.

When I brought my own children home and began teaching with Charlotte Mason's principles, I felt — for the first time — like I was doing what literature is actually for. We read aloud. We narrated. We argued about whether Heathcliff was a monster or a man destroyed by the world around him. We had real conversations.

Delight & Savor is my attempt to give other families — and other co-op classrooms — that same experience. I hold a master's in Literary Studies. I teach these materials weekly with real high school students. What you're getting isn't theoretical. It's field-tested, student-proven, and built with care.
