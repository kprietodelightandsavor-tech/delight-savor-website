Delight & Savor ✦ In the Margin

A look inside the work

Before you begin, see how your reader learns to notice, narrate, and write.

Notice · Narrate · Connect · Write

In the Margin is built on a simple, old idea: children grow as writers when they first learn to pay attention. Reading comes first, then telling it back in their own words — and only then the slow, satisfying work of shaping what they noticed into thought.

Here are the two pieces families ask to see most. The Writing Table below is fully live — tap through it. The Capstone shows the shape of a whole term’s work. Then, just below, the units themselves.

The Writing Table

From what stayed with you, to what you want to say.

“Before we build an argument, we begin by noticing what lingers.”

A calm, guided notebook that carries a student from a first impression all the way to a finished essay outline — one quiet movement at a time. No blank page, no overwhelm. Just the next honest question.

  • Narration — tell it back in your own words
  • Claim — what do you want to argue?
  • Evidence — the moments that prove it
  • Analysis — what the text reveals
  • Connections — joining the great conversation
  • So What — why this matters, in life
  • Revision — the outline, ready to write

Everything saves on its own as they go, and the finished outline prints or copies in a single tap.

The Capstone

Following a thread through the term

One symbol, one idea — followed from the first page to the last.

Each term, a student carries a single thread through everything they read, meeting its thinkers, themes, and language through many different doors — the eight intelligences Howard Gardner described — and climbing the levels of Bloom’s taxonomy, from understanding toward analysis and making something new.

The assignments shift from term to term — summer runs six weeks, fall and spring thirteen to sixteen — but every term builds toward two things: a Composition Exam (a literary analysis with a works cited) and the Quilt (a 4×4 watercolor or art piece of the thread they followed, with a quote on the back that holds it in words, and a brief explanation).

Offered each term, for the live course and self-paced families alike.

Come read alongside us.

What your child makes here is small, and it is theirs, and it is real. That is the whole point.

Which Version Is Right for You?

Co-op vs. Home Study

Every unit comes in two versions — one designed for a weekly co-op classroom, one for the independent home student. The content and rigor are the same; the structure and pacing differ.

i

Co-op Version

For weekly classroom use

  • Designed for 1.5-hour weekly sessions
  • Includes full teacher's guide with discussion prompts, timing notes, and gray-box teacher annotations
  • Student handouts formatted for in-class narration and discussion
  • Absent student guides so no one falls behind
  • Oral narration as the backbone — writing builds gradually across the semester
  • Honors Track extensions for students ready to go deeper
ii

Home Study Version

For independent learners

  • Flexible pacing — complete each week's work in 2–3 sessions
  • Student-facing handouts designed for independent reading and narration
  • Parent/teacher guide with light facilitation notes
  • Written narration options included for every week
  • Same analytical method and thesis progression as the co-op version
  • Family license — covers all students in one household

Available in Both Versions

The Honors Track

Each unit includes optional Honors Track extensions — deeper reading, additional secondary sources, and more demanding writing prompts — for the student who is ready to be genuinely challenged. The Honors Track is woven into the same handouts; no separate purchase needed.
