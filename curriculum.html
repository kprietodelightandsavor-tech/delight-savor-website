Year One

Tragedy & Gothic

Macbeth · Wuthering Heights · Grades 9–12

Available Now Honors Track Included Co-op + Home

Fall · Macbeth

Spring · Wuthering Heights

Year One opens with two of the most dramatic and morally complex texts in the English canon — Shakespeare's Macbeth in the fall and Emily Brontë's Wuthering Heights in the spring. Students encounter ambition, grief, coercive love, and the consequences of disordered desire through whole, unabridged texts.

Through the lens of Augustine's ordo amoris and Aristotle's virtue ethics, students don't just read these stories — they think through them philosophically. What does Macbeth love, and in what order? What has Heathcliff disordered, and why? These are not comprehension questions. They are real questions worth arguing about.

Writing develops through the Little Red Writing Book, working through a few principles each term — building craft slowly, with ideas always in front of technique.

Texts

  • Macbeth — William Shakespeare
  • Wuthering Heights — Emily Brontë
  • Little Red Writing Book (select principles per term)

Key Thinkers

  • Augustine — Ordo Amoris
  • Aristotle — Virtue & Habit
  • Rousseau — Nature & Civilization

By the end of Year One, students will be able to…

  • Narrate complex literary scenes orally and in writing with confidence
  • Trace a philosophical lens (Augustine, Aristotle) across an entire novel
  • Write a thesis-driven literary argument with textual evidence
  • Engage in Socratic seminar discussion at a high school level
  • Identify Gothic and tragic conventions and explain their effect

☀ A Seasonal Offering

The Summer Table:
A Reading Collective

Each summer, a focused, unhurried course built around a single text — a chance to read slowly, discuss deeply, and arrive at the next school year with something already alive in the mind.

Current Offering

Coming Soon

The Summer Table is not test prep or a head start on the fall. It's a reading collective — a small group of students and a teacher who gather around a book that deserves more time than the school year usually gives it.

The current summer offering is built around Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men — a short novel with enormous moral weight, perfect for a summer when students have space to sit with a hard question: what do we owe each other?

The Summer Table changes each year. Join the newsletter to be notified when the next offering is announced.

The Summer Table is designed to work whether or not a student has taken any Delight & Savor courses. It stands alone — welcoming, accessible, and genuinely worth the summer.

Year Two

Art of Attention

Poetry + Old Man and the Sea · A Midsummer Night's Dream · Grades 9–12

Coming Soon Honors Track Included

Fall · Poetry & Old Man

Spring · Midsummer

Coming Soon

Year Two materials are currently in development. Join the waitlist to be notified when they're available.

Year Two asks a single central question: what does it mean to truly pay attention? In the fall, students read poets who model attentiveness — Berry, Frost, Dickinson, Williams, Oliver, Hopkins — alongside Hemingway's The Old Man and the Sea.

In the spring, Shakespeare's A Midsummer Night's Dream turns the question sideways: what happens when we stop paying attention, when desire clouds perception? Together the year forms a meditation on seeing — carefully, honestly, with love.

Texts

  • Poetry — Berry, Frost, Dickinson, Williams, Oliver, Hopkins
  • The Old Man and the Sea — Hemingway
  • A Midsummer Night's Dream — Shakespeare
  • Little Red Writing Book (continued)

Key Thinkers

  • Simone Weil — Attention as Love
  • Charlotte Mason — Science of Relations
  • Aristotle — Perception & Form

Year Three bridges the medieval and the modern. Chaucer's Canterbury Tales opens the year with the full range of human folly and grace. The spring moves into the modern short story — from Welty and Chekhov to Kafka and Le Guin. The writing companion is Zinsser's On Writing Well.

Texts

  • Canterbury Tales — Chaucer
  • Short stories — Welty, Chekhov, Melville, Sartre, Kafka, Le Guin
  • On Writing Well — Zinsser

Key Themes

  • Chaucer's moral vision
  • Zinsser — Clarity & Voice
  • Existentialism & the absurd

Year Four is the year of comedy, irony, and the social novel. The central question: what does wit reveal about character? Students discover that comedy is not the absence of seriousness — it is seriousness in disguise.

Texts

  • Victorian social criticism
  • Comedy as a vehicle for truth
