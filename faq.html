Section One

About the Curriculum

  • Delight & Savor is designed for upper school students — grades 9 through 12. The units assume a student who is ready to read challenging literature independently, engage in substantive discussion, and begin developing written argument. Some advanced 8th graders have used the materials successfully; some 12th graders are still growing into them. The best guide is the student, not the grade.

  • DELIGHT is the analytical scaffold woven through every unit. Each letter names a kind of attention: Describe the Context, Explore, Look at the Language, Identify the Tone, Gather the Big Idea, Highlight the Quotation, Tie Together. Students encounter each move in practice before seeing the full framework — the goal is internalized habit, not memorized formula. The Teacher's Notebook has a full breakdown.

  • Each unit is 15 weeks. In a co-op setting, that's 15 weekly sessions of approximately 1.5 hours each. In a home study setting, students typically spend 3–4 hours per week on reading, narration, and handout work — spread across 2–3 sessions. The units are designed to fit a standard semester or half-year block.

  • The content and rigor are identical — the structure and pacing differ. The co-op version is built around a 1.5-hour weekly session: it includes a full teacher's guide with timing notes, discussion facilitation prompts, and gray-box teacher annotations. The home study version is paced for 2–3 sessions per week and formatted for independent work, with a lighter parent/teacher guide. Both versions include the Honors Track.

    See the versions comparison on the curriculum page for more detail.

  • The Honors Track is woven into the same handouts — no separate purchase needed. It includes additional secondary reading, more demanding writing prompts, and deeper analytical extensions for students who are ready to be genuinely stretched. Teachers and parents choose which students opt into the Honors Track; it's designed to be a commitment, not a casual add-on.

  • Yes. Delight & Savor units are literature companions — they are designed to be used alongside the primary text, not as a replacement for it. You will need a copy of Macbeth, Wuthering Heights, or whichever unit you are using. Standard editions work fine; the teacher's guide notes any edition-specific page references.

Section Two

Charlotte Mason

  • No. The units work beautifully for any family that values living books, real discussion, and writing that grows from genuine thought — regardless of whether you formally follow the Charlotte Mason method. The teacher's guides explain the approach so that no prior CM training is required. That said, if you're curious to go deeper, I recommend Charlotte Mason's own A Philosophy of Education and Karen Glass's Consider This.

  • Narration is the student's own re-telling — in their own words, from their own memory — of what they encountered in the text. It is not a summary, not a quiz, not a comprehension check. Charlotte Mason called it "the art of knowing." It reveals what actually landed for a student, as opposed to what we told them to notice.

    At the upper level, narration becomes the seedbed for written argument. A student who has narrated faithfully for several weeks has been gathering the raw material they will need to write a thesis — without knowing it. The Teacher's Notebook walks through how narration works in a co-op session.

  • Charlotte Mason and the classical tradition share more than they differ on — both prioritize the formation of the whole person, both trust students to engage with difficult primary sources, both see education as initiation into the Great Conversation. Delight & Savor draws on both streams: the CM emphasis on narration and living books, and the classical emphasis on rhetoric, logic, and great texts. Students who use these materials alongside a classical co-op often find the approaches reinforce each other beautifully.

Section Four

Getting Started

  • If you are starting fresh, begin with Macbeth (Year One, Fall). It is the unit most explicitly designed to introduce the DELIGHT framework and establish the narration habit from scratch. Wuthering Heights assumes students have some familiarity with the approach, though it works as a standalone unit for experienced readers.

  • For experienced teachers: 30–45 minutes per week, primarily reading the teacher's guide and the assigned text sections. For teachers new to Charlotte Mason or the specific text: plan for 1–1.5 hours the first few weeks as you get oriented, dropping as the semester progresses. The teacher's guides are written to minimize prep — everything you need to facilitate the session is there.

  • Yes — and this approach may be exactly what they need. Most writing struggles at the upper level are actually thinking struggles: the student doesn't know what they want to say, so they can't write it. Narration addresses this at the root. A student who spends ten weeks narrating faithfully will have something to say by the time we ask them to write it. The progression from oral narration to written argument is gradual enough that even reluctant writers rarely feel ambushed by the final essay.
